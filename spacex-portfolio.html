An Informal Message

I have always been inspired by space exploration; it is my belief that exploration is the most natural thing that comes to humans as a species.

Humans are at their best when they've climbed one peak, only to see the next one in the distance and feel the urge to keep going. That is the mentality that takes humans to space, to Mars, and eventually, beyond.

Someday, I would like to see Earth's entirety with my own eyes and perhaps even step on the surface of Mars. It is the whole reason why I study to become an aerospace engineer. I strive to build beautiful things, climb those mountains, and "boldly go where no man has gone before."

I am deeply honored to be considered for this internship and I am excited to show off some of my hobbies, projects, and work samples.

Thank you for your time.

- Thomas

ESP32 Presence Monitoring System

Developed an ESP32-based monitoring system for the WPI Wireless Association's space, "The Shack," to detect occupancy, control an ON AIR sign, and post occupancy status updates through Slack to encourage member usage of the space. The project combined embedded sensing, relay control, and deployment in a shared technical space.

System elements
  • ESP32 microcontroller
  • Light sensor based detection logic
  • Relay-controlled signage
  • Slack API integration
  • 3D printed enclosure and hardware
Design considerations
  • Long term operation and reliability
  • False-trigger reduction through moving average
  • Simple interface for club members

IQP: Remote Sensing, Excel, and ArcGIS Applications

WPI Interactive Qualifying Project with The Greater Wellington Regional Council focused on evaluating and quantifying forest restoration progress across regional parks in New Zealand. The project connected ecological monitoring, spreadsheet-based data cleaning, ArcGIS visualization, asset-management logic, and future remote sensing workflows.

My contributions

  • GIS workflow across ~343 hectares
  • Analyzed forest data, five parks
  • Researched carbon credit policy
  • Created regional park analysis map
Remote sensing research
  • Drone imagery for canopy mapping
  • LiDAR for canopy-height estimation
  • AI-assisted stem counting methods
  • Cleaned contractor survival-audit data
  • Visualized survival and mortality rates
  • Recommended future data-collection workflows

IEEE Robotics Compliant Gripper

Led the mechanical design of a compliant gripping mechanism for an IEEE robotics competition. The design used a single motor to reduce software and sensor complexity, solving the gripping problem through mechanical ingenuity rather than additional electronics.

Design highlights
  • Single-motor compliant mechanism
  • Custom shear-pin fail-safe assembly
  • Fails safely under excess torque
  • Rapid reset for competition reuse
Fabrication and iteration
  • CAD modeling and technical documentation
  • Manual machining
  • 3D printed prototypes
  • Iterated under competition-driven constraints

The gripper in action, the compliant gripping mechanism, and the shear-pin collar and retainer.

Annual Fireworks Display

Every year I put on a fireworks show for friends and family. It started as a small hobby and has grown into something the whole neighborhood looks forward to: nearly 2,000 people came out to watch last year. I love picking out the shells, planning how the night will unfold, and watching everyone's reaction when it all comes together. There is something about the scale and the spectacle that has always drawn me in, and it is one of my favorite ways to cap off the year.

Lipscomb University Microhome

As part of Lipscomb University's Introduction to Engineering course in fall 2022, I worked with thirteen fellow freshman mechanical engineering students to design and build a 12×7 ft microhome. The structure was engineered to run on either grid power or solar energy and was donated to Second Chance Ministries in Hurricane Mills, Tennessee, to provide emergency shelter for individuals displaced by the devastating 2021 floods in Waverly.

Engineering skills applied
  • Trigonometry for truss angle calculations
  • Structural framing and "building square" techniques
  • Material selection and procurement
  • Dual-power system (grid and solar)
Design adaptations
  • Mothers with infants
  • Homeless students
  • Individuals with visual impairment
  • Wheelchair users

Faculty: Dr. David Elrod and Dr. Kirsten Dodson, Lipscomb University, Raymond B. Jones College of Engineering. Donated to Second Chance Ministries, founded by Shirley James.
